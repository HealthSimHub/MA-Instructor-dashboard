Note on Sandra vs Marcus: Both are Medicare. Marcus is specifically Part B (outpatient/physician services). Sandra is listed as Medicare general — accept either Part A or general Medicare for Sandra. If a student says Part B for both, ask them to explain the difference.

BONUS: Medicare Part A vs Part B

Medicare Part A covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, hospice, and some home health care. It is generally premium-free for most people who paid Medicare taxes.

Medicare Part B covers outpatient services — physician visits, preventive care, lab tests, durable medical equipment, and outpatient procedures. Part B requires a monthly premium.

Simple anchor: Part A = Admitted to hospital. Part B = Beside the doctor in the office.

Coding 101 — Answer Key
ICD-10-CM · CPT · HCPCS + rationale
MOD 12
Grading note: Students must identify the correct code TYPE and provide a logical rationale. They do not need to provide the actual code number — code selection is a separate skill. Award partial credit if the type is correct but rationale is weak.
#SITUATIONCODE TYPEWHY?
1 Provider diagnoses Marcus with Type 2 Diabetes, uncontrolled. ICD-10-CM This is a diagnosis — the reason for the visit. ICD-10-CM codes always describe what is wrong with the patient. (Approx. code: E11.65)
2 Provider performs a comprehensive office visit for a new patient (Destiny's sports physical). CPT This is a procedure/service performed by the provider. CPT codes describe what was done during the visit. (Approx. code: 99205 — new patient, comprehensive)
3 Robert is ordered a portable oxygen concentrator for home use. HCPCS Durable Medical Equipment (DME) is billed using HCPCS Level II codes. This is the classic HCPCS example — equipment not captured in CPT. (Approx. code: E1390)
4 Marcus is also diagnosed with essential hypertension. ICD-10-CM Again a diagnosis — a second condition being documented. Multiple ICD-10-CM codes can be used in a single encounter. (Approx. code: I10)
5 Provider orders a 12-lead EKG for Robert. CPT An EKG is a diagnostic procedure performed during the visit. CPT codes describe procedures and services rendered. (Approx. code: 93000)
6 The clinic administers a flu shot to Destiny at checkout. HCPCS Vaccine administration and the vaccine product itself use HCPCS codes. The flu vaccine is not in CPT — it requires both a HCPCS product code and a CPT administration code. This is the most commonly missed question — use it for class discussion.

Medical necessity — model answer:

Medical necessity means the service or procedure performed must be clinically justified by the patient's diagnosis. The ICD-10-CM diagnosis code must support the CPT procedure code — if the codes don't match logically, the insurance company will deny the claim for lack of medical necessity. Example: billing a cardiac stress test for a patient whose only diagnosis is a sprained ankle would be denied because there is no medical reason connecting the two.

Phone Screening Role Play — Model Answers
Sample scripts + what to listen for when evaluating
MOD 8PARTNER ACTIVITY
📞 THE SCENARIO

CALLER: "Hi, I'm calling to make an appointment for my dad. He's been having some chest tightness and he's a little short of breath when he walks to the mailbox. It's been happening for about two days. He's 68, he's a veteran, and I want to get him in as soon as possible."

MODEL OPENING:

"Thank you for calling [Clinic Name], this is [Name] speaking. How can I help you today?" — OR — "Good morning, [Clinic Name], [Name] speaking. How may I assist you?"

Key elements: clinic name, MA name, professional tone, open-ended invitation. Do NOT say "What do you want?"

REQUIRED SCREENING QUESTIONS (students should ask ALL of these):

  • What is the patient's full name?
  • What is the patient's date of birth?
  • What is the reason for the visit / chief complaint? (already given, but confirm: "Can you describe the symptoms in more detail?")
  • How long have the symptoms been present? (already given — 2 days)
  • Is the patient currently experiencing symptoms? Is the pain/discomfort happening right now?
  • What is the patient's insurance? (caller mentioned veteran — confirm TRICARE)
  • Is the patient established with this practice or a new patient?
  • What is the best callback number?

TRIAGE DECISION — model answer:

This is an urgent same-day appointment. Chest tightness + shortness of breath in a 68-year-old = cardiac red flags. The MA should not schedule this as a routine appointment. Rationale: symptoms are ongoing (2 days), patient is elderly, symptoms worsen with activity — all indicators of potential cardiac involvement requiring prompt evaluation.

THE PLOT TWIST — "He says the pain is getting worse and he feels dizzy now":

Do NOT put the caller on hold. Stay on the line.

Model response: "I hear you — I need you to stay on the line with me. Based on what you're describing, I want you to call 911 right now or have someone drive him to the emergency room immediately. Do not wait for an office appointment. Is there someone with him right now who can help?"

What to look for in student responses:
  • ✓ Did NOT put caller on hold
  • ✓ Recognized escalation as emergency — not routine office visit
  • ✓ Directed to 911 or ER — not "come in when you can"
  • ✓ Stayed calm, did not panic
  • ✓ Confirmed someone is with the patient
  • ✗ Do not accept "I'll get you scheduled ASAP" as a sufficient response
Grading note for role play: This activity is performance-based — grade on the 4 criteria in the rubric (screening questions, triage decision, emergency response, professional tone). The written script is a planning tool; the actual role play is the assessment. Listen for the plot twist response especially — this is the CCMA exam question.

Why verify insurance at every visit — model answer:

Insurance coverage can change at any time — employer changes, loss of job, birthday rule changes, Medicare enrollment periods, Medicaid redeterminations. If the clinic provides services and the patient's insurance has lapsed or changed, the clinic may not be reimbursed and the patient may owe the full amount unexpectedly. Verifying at every visit protects the patient AND the clinic. This is also a compliance requirement for Medicare and Medicaid providers.

Denied Claim Decision Tree — Answer Key
Correct next step + rationale for all 5 denial scenarios
MOD 13
Grading note: Students must identify the correct ACTION and explain WHY. The rationale matters as much as the answer — this is how CCMA exam questions are written.
#DENIAL REASONCORRECT NEXT STEPRATIONALE
1 "Lack of medical necessity" Submit an appeal with supporting documentation Verify diagnosis and procedure codes are correct and support each other. Attach clinical notes, lab results, or provider documentation that supports why the service was necessary. Submit a formal appeal — do NOT resubmit the original claim or bill the patient first.
2 "Patient not eligible on date of service" Verify eligibility, correct if error, then resubmit or bill patient First check: was eligibility verified before the visit? If the MA failed to verify, this is an internal error. If insurance lapsed, the patient may be responsible for the bill. If it was an insurance error (coverage was actually active), appeal with proof of eligibility on date of service.
3 "Service requires prior authorization" Obtain retroactive prior authorization if possible, then resubmit Contact the insurance company to request retroactive prior auth — some plans allow this, some do not. If denied retroactively, submit an appeal explaining the clinical urgency. Going forward: always check prior auth requirements before scheduling the service. This denial is largely preventable.
4 "Duplicate claim submission" Do NOT resubmit — submit a claim adjustment instead Resubmitting creates another duplicate denial. The correct action is to submit a claim adjustment or corrected claim with a note explaining the situation. This is the most common student error — "just resubmit it" is always wrong for a duplicate denial.
5 "Invalid diagnosis code" Correct the ICD-10-CM code and resubmit as a corrected claim Look up the correct, most specific ICD-10-CM code for the diagnosis. Verify with the provider if unsure. Resubmit as a corrected claim (not a new claim) with the updated code. Also check that the new code supports the procedure codes billed.

Clearinghouse — model answer:

A clearinghouse is a third-party company that acts as an intermediary between the healthcare provider and the insurance company. The provider submits claims to the clearinghouse, which checks them for errors (scrubbing), formats them correctly for each payer, and transmits them electronically. The clearinghouse also receives remittance advice back from the payer and routes it to the provider. Using a clearinghouse reduces claim errors and speeds up reimbursement.

Remittance Advice (RA) — model answer:

Remittance Advice (RA) is the document sent by the insurance company to the provider after processing a claim. It explains what was paid, what was denied, and why. It includes the patient's name, date of service, billed amount, allowed amount, payment amount, and any adjustment codes explaining denials or reductions. The MA uses the RA to post payments to the patient's account and identify claims that need to be appealed or billed to the patient.

Model answers for all 6 short-answer questions

Grading note: These are short answer — students do not need to match the model exactly. Award full credit for answers that capture the key concept accurately. Award partial credit for answers that are partially correct but missing a critical element.

1. What is an ABN (Advance Beneficiary Notice of Noncoverage)?

An ABN is a form given to Medicare beneficiaries when the provider believes a service may NOT be covered by Medicare because it might not meet medical necessity requirements. The patient signs the ABN to acknowledge they understand they may be responsible for paying for the service out of pocket if Medicare denies it. Key point: it must be given BEFORE the service is performed — not after. It only applies to fee-for-service Medicare, not Medicare Advantage plans.

2. What is a superbill / encounter form?

A superbill (also called an encounter form) is a document generated for each patient visit that captures the services performed and diagnoses documented during that encounter. It includes: patient demographics, date of service, provider name, diagnosis codes (ICD-10-CM), procedure codes (CPT/HCPCS), fees, and insurance information. It serves as the source document for billing — the billing department uses it to submit the insurance claim.

3. What is the difference between an EOB and Remittance Advice?

Both documents explain how a claim was processed, but they go to different recipients:

EOB (Explanation of Benefits) — sent to the patient by their insurance company. It shows what was billed, what insurance paid, and what the patient owes. It is NOT a bill.

Remittance Advice (RA) — sent to the provider/clinic by the insurance company. It contains the same information but is used by the billing department to post payments and identify denials.

4. What is an A/R Aging Report and why run it weekly?

An Accounts Receivable (A/R) Aging Report shows all outstanding (unpaid) claims organized by how long they have been unpaid — typically in columns: 0–30 days, 31–60 days, 61–90 days, 90+ days. The older the debt, the harder it is to collect. Clinics run it weekly to prioritize follow-up on the oldest unpaid claims before they reach timely filing deadlines, and to identify patterns in which payers are consistently slow or denying claims.

5. What is precertification? Give an example.

Precertification (also called prior authorization or preauthorization) is the process of getting approval from the insurance company BEFORE performing a service or procedure. The provider must submit clinical documentation justifying why the service is medically necessary, and the insurer reviews it before approving or denying.

Example: Marcus Webb needs a referral to an endocrinologist. Before the referral is made, the MA contacts Medicare to obtain prior authorization, submitting documentation of his A1C of 9.2 and his diagnosis of uncontrolled T2DM as justification. Without it, the specialist visit may be denied.

6. What two identifiers are required at check-in and why?

The two required patient identifiers are: (1) Full legal name and (2) Date of birth. MRN (medical record number) is also acceptable as a second identifier.

Why: This is a patient safety requirement established by The Joint Commission and CMS to prevent wrong-patient errors — administering medications, ordering labs, or documenting in the wrong chart. Using two identifiers (not just name, which can be duplicated) ensures the MA is interacting with the correct patient before any clinical or administrative action is taken.
💡 CCMA Exam Note: Questions 1, 3, 5, and 6 are high-frequency CCMA exam topics. If students struggled with any of these, flag them for additional review before the certification exam. The ABN question and the two-identifier rule appear on almost every practice test.
